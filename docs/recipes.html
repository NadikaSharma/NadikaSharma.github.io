🍝 Butter Chicken Pasta

Pasta Indian-Fusion Comfort Food

This is my most-requested dish. The moment people try it, they always ask for the recipe — it's the perfect marriage of a classic Indian butter chicken and a cozy pasta dinner. The secret? Don't be shy with the kasoori methi and don't hold back on the cream. This dish is meant to be indulgent.

▶ VIEW FULL RECIPE

For the Chicken Marinade

  • 500g boneless skinless chicken breast or thighs, cut into bite-size cubes
  • 3 tbsp plain yogurt
  • 1 tbsp lemon juice
  • 1 tsp garlic powder
  • 1 tsp coriander powder
  • 1 tsp kosher salt
  • ½ tsp crushed pepper
  • ½ tsp turmeric powder
  • 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated

For the Butter Chicken Pasta

  • 2 tbsp olive oil
  • 2 tbsp butter
  • 1 tsp ground cumin
  • 1½ tsp garam masala
  • ½ tsp turmeric powder
  • 1 medium onion, chopped
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • 2 heaping tbsp kasoori methi (dried fenugreek leaves) — don't skimp on this!
  • 1 tsp coriander powder
  • ½ tsp cayenne pepper powder (adjust to heat preference)
  • 1½ cups passata or ready-made tomato pasta sauce
  • ¼ cup water
  • 1 cup heavy cream — be generous
  • 1 tsp honey
  • 300g fusilli pasta, cooked
  • Fresh cilantro, chopped (for garnish)

Instructions

  1. Marinate the chicken. Combine the chicken with all marinade ingredients in a large bowl. Mix well, cover, and refrigerate for at least 1 hour — overnight if you can. This is where most of the flavor and moisture comes from, so don't rush it.
  2. Cook the chicken. Heat olive oil in a heavy-bottomed pan over medium-high heat. Add the marinated chicken in a single layer and cook for 2 minutes, flip, and cook for another 2–3 minutes. Remove and set aside.
  3. Build the sauce. In the same pan, melt the butter. Add cumin, garam masala, and turmeric — stir until fragrant. Add the onion and garlic, cook until soft and translucent. Then add salt, pepper, kasoori methi, coriander powder, and cayenne. Stir well.
  4. Simmer. Add the cooked chicken and passata. Stir, cover, and simmer on low heat for 10 minutes, stirring occasionally.
  5. Finish with cream. Pour in the heavy cream and honey. Taste and adjust seasoning. Add the cooked pasta and toss everything together over heat for 1–2 more minutes.
  6. Garnish with fresh cilantro and serve immediately.

💛 Nadika's tip: Kasoori methi is non-negotiable in this recipe — it's what gives it that authentic butter chicken depth. Rub it between your palms before adding it to release the oils. And please, use more cream than you think you need. You won't regret it.

🍰 Rasmalai Tres Leches Cake

Dessert South Asian-Fusion Make Ahead

This is my love letter to two of the best desserts in the world. Rasmalai is one of my all-time favorite South Asian sweets — that cold, cardamom-saffron soaked milk is pure comfort. When you combine those flavors with a tres leches cake, something magical happens. The key is patience: let the milk simmer low and slow, and then pour every last drop into the cake. Serve it cold — always cold.

▶ VIEW FULL RECIPE

For the Cake

  • A generous pinch of saffron strands
  • 3 tbsp whole milk, warm (to bloom the saffron)
  • 4 eggs, separated
  • ¼ tsp cream of tartar
  • 80g granulated sugar (for egg whites)
  • 80g granulated sugar (for egg yolks)
  • 1½ tsp cardamom powder
  • 120g plain (all-purpose) flour
  • 1 tsp baking powder
  • ¼ tsp salt

For the Rasmalai Milk Mixture

  • 1½ cups whole milk
  • 8–10 cardamom pods, lightly crushed
  • A pinch of saffron
  • 1 can (400ml) condensed milk
  • 1 can (400ml) evaporated milk

For the Whipped Cream Topping

  • 300ml double (heavy) cream
  • 2 tbsp icing (powdered) sugar
  • 1 tsp vanilla extract
  • Chopped pistachios, for topping
  • A pinch of saffron, for garnish

Instructions

  1. Bloom the saffron. Add saffron strands to the warm milk and set aside for 10 minutes.
  2. Make the cake batter. Whip egg whites with cream of tartar until foamy, then gradually add 80g sugar and beat to stiff peaks. In a separate bowl, whisk egg yolks with remaining 80g sugar until pale and thick. Stir in the saffron milk and cardamom. Fold in flour, baking powder, and salt. Gently fold in the egg whites in three additions.
  3. Bake. Pour into an ungreased 9-inch square tin. Bake at 175°C (350°F) for 22–25 minutes. Do not grease the tin — the batter needs to grip the sides to rise. As soon as it comes out of the oven, flip it upside down onto a wire rack (still in the tin) and cool for 20 minutes.
  4. Make the rasmalai milk — low and slow. Combine whole milk, crushed cardamom pods, and saffron in a saucepan. Bring to a gentle simmer over low heat and let it cook slowly for 20–25 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the milk is fragrant and slightly reduced. Don't rush this — the slow simmer is what builds that deep rasmalai flavor. Strain, then mix with condensed milk and evaporated milk.
  5. Soak the cake. Poke holes all over the cake with a skewer. Pour the milk mixture slowly, a little at a time — let each addition fully absorb before adding more. Use every last drop. The cake should be completely saturated.
  6. Chill. Refrigerate for at least 4 hours, preferably overnight.
  7. Top and serve cold. Whip cream with icing sugar and vanilla to soft peaks. Spread over the cold cake. Garnish with pistachios and saffron. Serve straight from the fridge — this cake must be eaten cold.

💛 Nadika's tip: The slow-cooked milk is everything. Give it time on low heat and your kitchen will smell absolutely incredible. And when you're pouring the milk into the cake — be patient, keep going, use it all. The more it absorbs, the better it gets. This cake is even better the next day.

🍜 Thukpa (Nepali Noodle Soup)

Nepali Soup Cozy

Thukpa is a warm hug in a bowl. It's a noodle soup that originates from the Himalayan regions and is deeply comforting — the kind of thing you crave on a cold day or when you're missing home. Growing up in Kathmandu, this was always a staple, and making it now feels like bringing a little piece of Nepal into my kitchen.

▶ VIEW FULL RECIPE

Ingredients (serves 3–4)

  • 200g flat wheat noodles or egg noodles
  • 300g boneless chicken, thinly sliced (or vegetables for a vegetarian version)
  • 2 tbsp oil
  • 1 medium onion, finely chopped
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated
  • 2 medium tomatoes, chopped
  • 1 carrot, julienned
  • 1 cup cabbage, thinly shredded
  • 2 stalks green onion, chopped
  • 1 tsp cumin powder
  • ½ tsp turmeric powder
  • 1 tsp coriander powder
  • ½ tsp chili powder (adjust to taste)
  • Salt to taste
  • 4 cups chicken broth (or vegetable broth)
  • 1 tbsp soy sauce
  • Fresh cilantro and a squeeze of lemon, to serve

Instructions

  1. Cook the noodles according to package instructions. Drain, toss with a little oil to prevent sticking, and set aside.
  2. Sauté the aromatics. Heat oil in a large pot over medium heat. Add onion and cook until golden. Add garlic and ginger, stir for 1 minute until fragrant.
  3. Add spices and tomatoes. Add cumin, turmeric, coriander, and chili powder. Stir for 30 seconds. Add the tomatoes and cook until they soften and break down, about 5 minutes.
  4. Cook the chicken. Add the sliced chicken and cook until no longer pink, about 4–5 minutes.
  5. Build the broth. Pour in the chicken broth and soy sauce. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a simmer. Add carrots and cabbage, and cook for another 5–7 minutes until vegetables are tender.
  6. Assemble. Divide noodles into bowls. Ladle the hot soup and vegetables over the noodles. Top with green onions and fresh cilantro. Finish with a squeeze of lemon.

💛 Nadika's tip: Thukpa is incredibly forgiving — use whatever vegetables you have on hand. The lemon squeeze at the end is not optional in my opinion; it brightens the whole bowl. And if you want to make it more Nepali-style, serve it with a side of achar (pickle) for a little kick.
